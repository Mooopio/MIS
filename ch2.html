📦 Business Processes — Foundations ▼

A business process = a set of logically related activities/steps that produce a specific business outcome. Involves flows of material, information, and knowledge.

  • Assembling the product
  • Managing inventory
  • Controlling quality

  • Managing cash flow

  • Evaluating performance
  • Enrolling in benefits

⚙️ Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) ▼

DEFINITION
Performs and records daily routine transactions necessary to conduct business.

  • Serves operational managers and staff
  • Examples: sales order entry, payroll, shipping, hotel reservations
  • Allows monitoring of operations status
  • Serves predefined, structured goals and decision making
🧾 Payroll TPS Example
Time Card (Input)
→
TPS Processing
→
Paychecks + Reports

Captures employee payment data → outputs hard-copy & online reports for management

🧠 Memory Hook
TPS = "Totally Predictable Stuff"
TPS handles repetitive, structured, daily transactions. No analysis. Just recording.

📊 Management Information Systems (MIS) ▼

SERVES Middle Management

  • Provides reports on firm's current performance
  • Data sourced from TPS (summarized)
  • Answers routine questions with predefined procedures
  • Little analytic capability — reports only
📋 MIS Data Flow
TPS 1 (Sales)
TPS 2 (Payroll)
TPS 3 (Inventory)
↓
MIS Reporting System
↓

🧠 Memory Hook
MIS = "My Information Summary"
Middle managers get a summary of what happened. No what-ifs. No analysis. Just facts.
🔮 Decision Support Systems (DSS) ▼

SERVES Middle Management

  • Supports non-routine decision making
  • Uses both internal (TPS/MIS) + external data
  • Example: "If December sales doubled, what happens to production schedule?"
  • Model-Driven DSS: Voyage-estimating systems (shipping bids)
  • Data-Driven DSS: Intrawest marketing analysis
🚢 Voyage-Estimating DSS

Runs on powerful PC. Used daily by managers to develop bids on shipping contracts. Classic model-driven DSS example.

Key: DSS answers "what if" and "why" questions, not just "what happened."
🧠 Memory Hook
DSS = "Decisions Support Scenarios"
What-if analysis. Scenarios. Non-routine. Uses models AND data. Think flexibility.
👑 Executive Support Systems (ESS / EIS) ▼

SERVES Senior Management

  • Addresses non-routine decisions requiring judgment, evaluation, insight
  • Incorporates external events (tax laws, competitors)
  • Uses summarized data from MIS + DSS
  • Example: Digital Dashboard showing real-time firm financial performance
  • Displays: working capital, accounts receivable, accounts payable, cash flow, inventory
📱 Digital Dashboard

Real-time visual view of firm's health. Like a car dashboard for the CEO. Aggregates internal + external intelligence.

🧠 Memory Hook
ESS = "Executives See Synthesis"
Senior executives need the big picture — synthesized from everywhere, internal AND external.
🏢 Enterprise Applications — The 4 Giants ▼

Enterprise applications span functional areas, execute processes across the entire firm, and include all management levels. They may extend outside the organization.

1️⃣ Enterprise Systems (ERP)
  • Collects data from different firm functions
  • Stores in single central data repository
  • Resolves fragmented data problem
  • Enables coordination of daily activities
  • Helps with production, inventory, daily ops, and long-term planning
2️⃣ Supply Chain Management (SCM)
  • Manages relationships with suppliers
  • Shares: orders, production, inventory, delivery info
  • Goal: right product → right place → right time → lowest cost
  • Coordinates supply chain (often via Extranets)
3️⃣ Customer Relationship Management (CRM)
  • Coordinates business processes dealing with customers
  • Covers: Sales, Marketing, Customer Service
  • Helps identify, attract, and retain most profitable customers
4️⃣ Knowledge Management Systems (KMS)
  • Captures and applies knowledge & expertise
  • How to create, produce, deliver products/services
  • Collects internal knowledge → makes available to employees
  • Links to external knowledge sources
🧠 Mnemonic — 4 Enterprise Apps
E-S-C-K = "Every Smart Company Knows"
Enterprise → Supply Chain → CRM → Knowledge Management
🌐 Intranets, Extranets & E-Business ▼
🔒 Intranet

Internal company Web sites. Accessible only by employees. Used to increase integration and expedite information flow within the organization.

🔓 Extranet

Company Web sites accessible externally only to vendors and suppliers. Often used to coordinate the supply chain (connects to SCM).

🛒 E-Commerce

Buying and selling goods and services through the Internet. A subset of E-Business.

💼 E-Business

Use of digital technology and Internet to drive major business processes. Broader than E-Commerce.

🏛 E-Government

Using Internet technology to deliver information and services to citizens, employees, and businesses.

🧠 Scope Hierarchy
E-Commerce ⊂ E-Business ⊂ Digital Economy
E-Commerce is just buying/selling. E-Business is ALL digital-driven business processes. Exam trap: they are NOT the same!
🤝 Collaboration, Teamwork & Social Business ▼

DEFINITION Collaboration = working with others to achieve shared explicit goals.

Why Collaboration is Growing:

  • Changing nature of work
  • Growth of professional work — "interaction jobs"
  • Changing organization of the firm
  • Changing scope of the firm (global)
  • Emphasis on innovation
  • Changing culture of work

Benefits of Collaboration:

  • Productivity ↑
  • Quality ↑
  • Innovation ↑
  • Customer service ↑
  • Financial performance ↑ (profitability, sales, growth)
Requirements for Collaboration (Fig 2-7): Appropriate organizational structure AND culture + appropriate collaboration technology. Both are needed.

Social Business:

  • Uses social networking platforms (internal & external)
  • Engages employees, customers, and suppliers
  • Goal: deepen interactions + expedite information sharing
  • Creates "Conversations"
  • Requires information transparency — exchange without executive intervention

Command & Control vs. Collaborative Culture:

⛔ Command & Control

No value placed on teamwork. Lower-level employees don't participate in decisions. Top-down authority.

✅ Collaborative Culture
  • Senior managers rely on teams
  • Policies, products, systems rely on teams
  • Manager's purpose = build teams

TPS → MIS Connection

TPS captures raw daily transactions. MIS takes that data, summarizes it at period-end, and provides reports to middle managers. Without TPS, MIS has no data.

MIS + DSS → ESS Connection

ESS draws on summarized data from both MIS and DSS, PLUS external data (competitors, laws). ESS synthesizes everything for senior executives.

SCM ↔ Extranet Connection

SCM systems often use Extranets to coordinate with suppliers. The Extranet is the technical channel; SCM is the business process layer on top.

Collaboration ↔ Social Business

Social business IS collaboration, but extended with social networking platforms. It requires information transparency — a cultural AND technical requirement.

💀 Exam Traps Detector DANGER ZONE
⚠️ TRAP 1: MIS vs. DSS Confusion

❌ Wrong: "MIS supports non-routine decisions" or "DSS provides routine reports"

✅ Right: MIS = routine + structured + little analytic capability. DSS = non-routine + what-if + uses models.

⚠️ TRAP 2: E-Commerce = E-Business

❌ Wrong: Treating E-Commerce and E-Business as identical

✅ Right: E-Commerce is a SUBSET of E-Business. E-Business = all digital business processes. E-Commerce = only buying/selling online.

⚠️ TRAP 3: Intranet vs. Extranet

❌ Wrong: "Extranets are accessible by all employees" or "Intranets coordinate with suppliers"

✅ Right: Intranet = internal employees only. Extranet = external vendors/suppliers only.

⚠️ TRAP 4: ESS Data Source

❌ Wrong: "ESS uses only internal data" or "ESS is the same as MIS for senior managers"

✅ Right: ESS incorporates BOTH internal (from MIS + DSS) AND external data (tax laws, competitor info).

⚠️ TRAP 5: ERP vs. All Enterprise Apps

❌ Wrong: Calling ERP "the enterprise application" — there are 4 types!

✅ Right: Enterprise Applications include 4: Enterprise Systems (ERP), SCM, CRM, and KMS. ERP is just one of them.

⚠️ TRAP 6: TPS Management Level

❌ Wrong: "TPS serves middle management"

✅ Right: TPS serves OPERATIONAL managers and staff — the lowest level. NOT middle management.

⚠️ TRAP 7: DSS = Only Models

❌ Wrong: "All DSS are model-driven"

✅ Right: DSS can be Model-Driven (voyage-estimating) OR Data-Driven (Intrawest marketing). Know both types!

⚠️ TRAP 8: Collaboration Only Needs Technology

❌ Wrong: "Good collaboration tools = good collaboration"

✅ Right: Collaboration requires BOTH appropriate organizational structure/culture AND technology. Culture is equally important.

📝 Essay: Explain the types of Information Systems and how they serve different management levels ▼
🔵 Introduction

Information systems are the backbone of modern organizations, designed to support decision-making at every level of management. Different management levels face different types of decisions, and thus require different types of information systems — ranging from operational transaction processing systems to strategic executive support systems.

📦 Body Point 1 — TPS (Operational Level)

At the operational level, Transaction Processing Systems (TPS) record and perform daily routine transactions such as payroll, sales order entry, and shipping. They serve operational managers and staff, providing structured, predefined outputs. For example, a payroll TPS captures time card data and produces employee paychecks and management reports.

📊 Body Point 2 — MIS (Middle Management)

Management Information Systems (MIS) serve middle management by providing summarized reports derived from TPS data. They answer routine questions through predefined procedures, such as producing an annual sales report. However, MIS have little analytic capability and cannot support complex decision-making.

🔮 Body Point 3 — DSS (Middle Management)

Decision Support Systems (DSS) also serve middle management but support non-routine, complex decisions. Unlike MIS, they can perform what-if analysis by integrating TPS, MIS, and external data. For instance, the voyage-estimating DSS helps shipping managers develop competitive bids by modeling various scenarios.

👑 Body Point 4 — ESS (Senior Management)

At the top level, Executive Support Systems (ESS) support senior management with strategic, non-routine decisions requiring judgment and insight. They synthesize internal data from MIS and DSS with external information about competitors and regulations. A digital dashboard is a prime example, giving executives a real-time view of financial performance indicators.

🔚 Conclusion

In conclusion, each type of information system is designed to match the nature of decisions at its management level. TPS handles structured operational tasks; MIS provides routine summaries; DSS enables analytical modeling; and ESS synthesizes intelligence for strategic decisions. Together, they form an integrated hierarchy that supports the entire organization.

📝 Essay: Explain the role of Enterprise Applications in improving organizational performance ▼
🔵 Introduction

Organizations face a major challenge: their activities span multiple departments, yet information is often fragmented across separate systems. Enterprise applications address this challenge by integrating processes and data across the entire firm, improving coordination, efficiency, and competitive performance.

📦 Body — Four Enterprise Applications

1. Enterprise Systems (ERP) collect data from all firm functions into a single central repository, eliminating data fragmentation and enabling real-time coordination. Managers can monitor operations and make both daily and long-term planning decisions effectively.

2. Supply Chain Management (SCM) systems manage relationships with suppliers by sharing real-time information about orders, inventory, and delivery. The goal is to deliver the right product to the right destination at the right time with the lowest possible cost.

3. Customer Relationship Management (CRM) systems coordinate all customer-facing processes — sales, marketing, and service — helping organizations identify, attract, and retain their most profitable customers.

4. Knowledge Management Systems (KMS) capture the organization's expertise and make it available to all employees, preventing knowledge loss and enabling consistent service delivery.

🔚 Conclusion

Enterprise applications transform isolated departmental systems into an integrated organizational intelligence. By breaking down information silos and connecting suppliers, operations, and customers, they enable firms to operate more efficiently, respond more quickly, and serve customers more effectively — directly improving organizational performance.

📝 Essay: Discuss the importance of collaboration and the role of technology in supporting it ▼
🔵 Introduction

In the modern business environment, no significant work is accomplished by individuals alone. Collaboration — working with others toward shared goals — has become a critical organizational capability, driven by fundamental changes in the nature of work and the structure of firms.

📦 Body Point 1 — Why Collaboration is Growing

Collaboration is increasingly important due to six key drivers: the changing nature of work, the growth of professional "interaction jobs," changing organizational structures, the expanding global scope of firms, the emphasis on innovation, and evolving workplace culture. These factors make teamwork not optional but essential.

📦 Body Point 2 — Business Benefits

Organizations that invest in collaboration technology see measurable returns: higher productivity, better quality outputs, increased innovation, improved customer service, and stronger financial performance including higher profitability and sales growth.

📦 Body Point 3 — Technology Tools

A wide range of technologies support collaboration, from simple email and instant messaging to sophisticated virtual meeting systems (telepresence), wikis, Microsoft SharePoint, and enterprise social networking platforms. These tools can be classified across two dimensions: location (same place vs. remote) and time (synchronous vs. asynchronous), forming the Time/Space Collaboration Matrix.

📦 Body Point 4 — Culture is Key

Technology alone is insufficient. Successful collaboration requires a collaborative organizational culture where senior managers value teams, policies are built through collective input, and the manager's primary role is to build and support teams — contrasting sharply with the traditional "command and control" structure.

🔚 Conclusion

Collaboration is no longer a luxury — it is a strategic necessity. Organizations must invest simultaneously in both the cultural transformation and the technological infrastructure needed to make teamwork effective. When both elements align, the business benefits are substantial and measurable.
